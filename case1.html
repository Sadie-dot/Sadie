Study: 1/5

Guiding client to a simpler design that actually fit their needs by asking goal-oriented questions

Client overlooked the user context.
How to win buy-in for a solution?

  • Solution required after request would negatively impact user experience & data integrity of their product.
  • I supervised review & deep rapport management to understand what was requested and why.
  • Imprecise requests & delay could result in failed to address prior to user acceptance testing (UAT).
  • To gain client confidence for streamlined user flow, I diagrammed pain points & clarified client need.

Collaboration across our teams in technology, medicine, and operations was critical.

👤

My role: Service design activities, including alignment with client context, optimizing user interactions for efficiency, and co-creating with internal & stakeholders.

The team: Solutions architect to write requirements, product delivery engineers, and clinical advisors on compliance & medical data.

Client: ████ a start-up. Nasdaq, $$$M revenue.

Core business: Developing medicines for rare diseases.

We finished design for the tracker & were set to build when client added medication use.

1. User confusion

New question was to be answered to right, conflicting with requirement for access plex; multiple times.

2. Imprecise data

Confirmation question asked about medication & count combined, not allowing for any difference in responses.

Harmonizing technical requirements with the unwieldy directive was my function.

1

1st Go | Self-discovery:

I attempted to help client discover for themselves why users could get flustered.

  • Engineers confirmed the user issue & flagged possible data inconsistencies, dooming project.
  • I then asked client several open-ended questions about how their response would work.
  • Client's responses indicated that they didn't yet recognize the incompatibility with their end goal.
2

2nd Go | Flow diagrams:

I diagrammed the flow after a solutions architect once used one to review specifications with me.

  • Enabled client to picture how user would move through if we implemented their instructions.
  • The problem I had described became real.
  • Client became invested in a solution.

The flow was snarled after much back-and-forth. Time for a simpler way.

★

Solution | Untangle & streamline:

I went back to basics to understand the need behind the task, then used medical questions for guidance.

  • Clinical team advised on medical questions for learning what data client required from users.
  • I discovered there were no technical or clinical limitations on splitting into 2 fit-for-purpose flows.
  • Client agreed we could split them, and there would be less burden on users who are caregivers of ill children.

The increased integrity of the design boosted client's 8-figure expansion plan.

  • More robust results will be achieved via efficiencies that encourage users to more frequently access the app.
  • Data quality improved once we expanded our assumptions.
  • Flow diagrams proved to be a useful communication tool equipping clients to make more satisfying decisions.
